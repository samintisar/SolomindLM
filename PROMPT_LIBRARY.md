# Prompt Library - Seed Prompts

Curated public prompts organized by studio tool.

## Report

### SMB Market Analysis
**Description:** Market insights, competitive positioning, and practical recommendations for small businesses.

**Prompt:**
> Create a market analysis report tailored for small and medium businesses. Focus on: (1) Market overview and key trends relevant to SMBs, (2) Competitive landscape and positioning opportunities, (3) Cost-effective strategies and tactics, (4) Practical implementation steps with realistic timelines, (5) Budget considerations and ROI estimates where possible, (6) Action items prioritized by impact and effort. Keep recommendations practical and achievable with limited resources.

## Flashcards

### Exam Prep Focus
**Description:** Flashcards targeting the most important concepts likely to appear on exams.

**Prompt:**
> Focus on creating flashcards for the most exam-worthy material. Prioritize: (1) Core definitions and key terminology, (2) Important formulas, equations, or models, (3) Key concepts that are frequently tested, (4) Relationships between concepts (cause-effect, compare-contrast), (5) Common misconceptions that trip students up. Create a balanced mix of recall, conceptual understanding, and application questions. Flag especially high-yield concepts.

---

### Vocabulary Builder
**Description:** Terminology and definition cards perfect for language learning or technical subjects.

**Prompt:**
> Create flashcards focused exclusively on vocabulary and terminology. For each term: (1) Provide a precise definition in your own words, (2) Include an example sentence or usage context, (3) Note any related terms or synonyms, (4) Flag whether the term is foundational (must know) or advanced (nice to know). Mix definition-style cards with fill-in-the-blank cards that test active recall of the term itself.

---

### Case Study & Application
**Description:** Scenario-based cards that test practical application of concepts to real-world situations.

**Prompt:**
> Create scenario-based flashcards that test application of concepts. Each card should present: (1) A realistic scenario or problem drawn from the source material, (2) A question asking what approach, method, or concept applies, (3) An answer explaining why that approach is correct and what the outcome would be. Focus on bridging theory to practice. Include process-oriented scenarios where students must identify the correct next step.

---

### Quick Review Essentials
**Description:** High-yield facts, formulas, and core concepts for rapid review before tests or meetings.

**Prompt:**
> Create a condensed set of high-yield flashcards containing only the absolute essentials. Include: (1) Key facts that must be memorized, (2) Critical formulas or equations with variables explained, (3) Core principles or laws, (4) Quick reference data points. Keep cards extremely concise. Optimize for rapid drilling. Avoid nuance or edge cases—focus on what matters most in 80% of situations.

## Quiz

### Concept Mastery Check
**Description:** Questions that test deep conceptual understanding, not just memorization.

**Prompt:**
> Create quiz questions that test deep conceptual understanding rather than surface-level recall. Focus on: (1) 'Why' and 'how' questions, not just 'what' questions, (2) Questions requiring students to compare and contrast approaches, (3) Questions about cause-and-effect relationships, (4) Questions that require synthesizing information across sections. Distractors should represent common misconceptions. Explanations should clarify the underlying concept, not just state the correct answer.

---

### Exam Simulation
**Description:** Mixed-difficulty questions covering all major topics comprehensively.

**Prompt:**
> Create a comprehensive exam simulation covering all major topics in the source material. Distribute questions evenly across topics. Include a mix of: (1) Easy recall questions (20%), (2) Medium conceptual questions (50%), (3) Hard analytical questions (30%). Ensure no topic is over-represented or under-represented. Questions should feel like they come from a well-designed final exam or certification test.

---

### Practical Problem Solving
**Description:** Scenario-based questions that require applying knowledge to solve realistic problems.

**Prompt:**
> Create practical problem-solving questions. Each question should present a realistic scenario and ask the student to: (1) Identify the correct approach or method, (2) Apply it to reach a solution, or (3) Evaluate which of several approaches is best and why. Scenarios should feel authentic to real-world applications of the material. Include questions where the answer requires multi-step reasoning.

---

### Knowledge Gap Diagnostic
**Description:** Questions designed to reveal weak areas and misconceptions.

**Prompt:**
> Create diagnostic questions designed to reveal knowledge gaps and misconceptions. Include: (1) Questions targeting common misunderstandings, (2) Questions that test edge cases and exceptions to rules, (3) Questions requiring distinction between similar concepts, (4) Questions where the 'obvious' answer is wrong. The goal is to surface what the student does NOT know. Explanations should directly address why wrong answers are tempting but incorrect.

## Slides

### Investor Pitch Deck
**Description:** Clean, data-driven slides optimized for pitching to investors or stakeholders.

**Prompt:**
> Create an investor pitch deck. Focus on: (1) Problem and market opportunity, (2) Solution and unique value proposition, (3) Market size and traction data, (4) Business model and revenue potential, (5) Competitive landscape and moats, (6) Team and execution plan, (7) The ask and use of funds. Use data-driven visuals, minimal text, and compelling narrative flow. Each slide should advance the investment case.

### Training & Onboarding Deck
**Description:** Educational slides with clear explanations, examples, and progressive complexity.

**Prompt:**
> Create a training presentation deck. Structure for learning: (1) Learning objectives upfront, (2) Foundational concepts with clear definitions, (3) Step-by-step processes with visual flow, (4) Concrete examples and case studies, (5) Common pitfalls and how to avoid them, (6) Practice scenarios or discussion questions, (7) Summary and next steps. Use progressive disclosure—start simple and build complexity. Include visual diagrams where helpful.

Use academic tone. Prioritize clarity of methodology and strength of evidence.

## Audio

### Friendly Study Companion
**Description:** Warm, encouraging audio that explains complex topics like a patient tutor.

**Prompt:**
> Create a friendly, encouraging audio explanation suitable for a student studying the material. The hosts should: (1) Explain complex ideas in plain language with relatable analogies, (2) Break down difficult concepts step by step, (3) Highlight what's most important to remember, (4) Share memory aids and mental models, (5) Encourage the listener and build confidence. Use a warm, conversational tone—like a knowledgeable friend explaining something over coffee. Avoid intimidating academic jargon unless necessary.

## Mindmap

### Concept Hierarchy
**Description:** Organize material into a hierarchical tree of concepts and sub-concepts.

**Prompt:**
> Create a concept hierarchy mind map. Organize the material into: (1) A single root topic representing the overarching theme, (2) 4-7 main branches for core concept categories, (3) Sub-branches for specific concepts within each category, (4) Fine-grained details as terminal nodes. Focus on conceptual relationships—how ideas relate to, depend on, or derive from each other. Avoid generic labels. Every node should be a specific concept from the source material.

---

### Process & Workflow Map
**Description:** Map out sequential processes, workflows, and cause-effect chains.

**Prompt:**
> Create a process-oriented mind map. Focus on: (1) Sequential steps in processes described in the source, (2) Decision points and branching paths, (3) Inputs and outputs at each stage, (4) Dependencies between steps, (5) Parallel processes that happen simultaneously. Structure as a flow-like hierarchy where order and causality are clear. Use action-oriented node names. Include feedback loops where the source describes iterative or cyclical processes.

---

### Compare & Contrast Map
**Description:** Structure material around comparisons, alternatives, and trade-offs.

**Prompt:**
> Create a compare-and-contrast mind map. Structure around: (1) Central topic or decision point, (2) Alternative approaches, methods, or perspectives as main branches, (3) Comparison dimensions (pros, cons, requirements, outcomes) as sub-branches, (4) Specific evidence from the source supporting each comparison. Highlight trade-offs and situational factors that make one option better than another in specific contexts. Make the comparison structure explicit and balanced.

---

### Research Synthesis
**Description:** Connect findings, evidence, and conclusions across multiple sources or sections.

**Prompt:**
> Create a research synthesis mind map. Focus on: (1) Central research question or thesis, (2) Key findings as main branches, (3) Evidence supporting each finding as sub-branches, (4) Connections between findings—how they reinforce, qualify, or contradict each other, (5) Methodologies used to generate evidence, (6) Gaps or limitations noted in the source. Emphasize the logical structure of the argument. Show how pieces of evidence build toward conclusions.

## Spreadsheet

### Structured Data Extraction
**Description:** Extract entities, methods, or concepts into a clean, sortable table.

**Prompt:**
> Extract distinct concepts, methods, or entities from the source into a structured table. For each item, capture: (1) Name/identifier, (2) Definition or description, (3) Key characteristics or properties, (4) Applications or contexts where it applies, (5) Relationships to other items. Consolidate examples under their parent concept rather than creating duplicate rows. Optimize for sorting and filtering.

---

### Comparison Matrix
**Description:** Side-by-side comparison of products, methods, theories, or approaches.

**Prompt:**
> Create a comparison matrix table. Identify the distinct items being compared (products, methods, theories, approaches) and extract: (1) Relevant comparison dimensions from the source (features, requirements, outcomes, constraints), (2) How each item performs on each dimension, (3) Pros and cons for each item, (4) Best-use scenarios. Ensure every item has a row and every relevant dimension has a column. Fill gaps with 'N/A' or 'Not mentioned' rather than inventing data.

---

### Timeline Tracker
**Description:** Chronological table of events, milestones, or developments.

**Prompt:**
> Create a chronological timeline table. Extract: (1) Dates or time periods mentioned, (2) Key events or milestones, (3) Description of what happened, (4) Significance or impact of each event, (5) People, organizations, or factors involved. Sort strictly chronologically from oldest to newest. Group related sub-events under their main event rather than creating separate rows. Include causal relationships where the source describes them.

---

### Financial Data Summary
**Description:** Extract financial figures, metrics, and budget data into a structured table.

**Prompt:**
> Create a financial data table. Extract: (1) Financial categories (revenue streams, expense types, accounts), (2) Item descriptions, (3) Amounts with consistent formatting, (4) Dates or periods associated with each figure, (5) Type (revenue, expense, asset, liability, metric). Preserve exact numbers from the source. Group related line items under their category. Include totals or subtotals if explicitly stated. Do not calculate new figures—only extract what the source provides.

## Written Questions

### Essay & Analysis Prompts
**Description:** Deep writing prompts requiring synthesis, argumentation, and critical analysis.

**Prompt:**
> Create essay prompts that require deep analytical writing. Each prompt should: (1) Present a complex question that cannot be answered with simple recall, (2) Require synthesis of multiple concepts or sections, (3) Ask for evaluation, comparison, or argumentation, (4) Provide clear point values and rubric criteria (argument strength, evidence use, organization, clarity). Include model answers that demonstrate the expected depth of analysis. Focus on higher-order thinking: analyze, evaluate, synthesize, argue.

---

### Short Answer Review
**Description:** Quick comprehension checks with specific, focused questions and clear model answers.

**Prompt:**
> Create short-answer questions for quick comprehension checks. Each question should: (1) Target a specific fact, definition, or concept, (2) Be answerable in 1-3 sentences, (3) Test whether the student grasped the essential meaning, not just memorized words, (4) Include a model answer with 2-3 specific keywords or facts required for full credit. Distribute questions evenly across all major topics. Mix definitional, procedural, and relational questions.

---

### Critical Thinking Challenges
**Description:** Questions that require evaluating evidence, identifying assumptions, and assessing arguments.

**Prompt:**
> Create critical thinking questions. Each prompt should ask the student to: (1) Evaluate the strength of evidence or arguments presented in the source, (2) Identify unstated assumptions or logical gaps, (3) Assess the validity of conclusions given the evidence, (4) Consider alternative explanations or counter-arguments. Include rubrics that reward analytical depth, not just correct recall. Model answers should demonstrate how to critically engage with the material rather than simply restate it.

---

### Application & Transfer
**Description:** Questions requiring students to apply concepts to novel situations and scenarios.

**Prompt:**
> Create application questions that test transfer of knowledge. Each prompt should: (1) Present a novel scenario not explicitly described in the source, (2) Ask the student to apply concepts from the material to analyze or solve the scenario, (3) Require connecting multiple concepts to reach a reasoned answer, (4) Include a detailed model answer showing the correct application step by step. Scenarios should feel realistic and relevant. The goal is to test whether students can use what they learned, not just describe it.
